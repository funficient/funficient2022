I'm Kate!

After spending years what felt like running around in circles getting nowhere slowly, I embarked on a personal journey to discover what it takes for people and teams to be more productive, make better decisions, and ultimately have a more fulfilling life.



Where it all started

While working with different teams in different organizations, I noticed patterns that kept teams from being productive. While people seemed busy all the time and all the tick-boxes were checked, releases were slow and error-prone.

People complained about what most felt were obvious, yet felt powerless to implement much-needed solutions. Customers didn't know how to articulate their needs until they could see something concrete. Business owners felt frustrated trying to steer a ship while everyone seemed to go in different directions. There was clearly no winner.

The outcome was demotivated employees, unhappy customers, mediocre products and a lot of waste.

It dawned on me that most teams weren't clear on their goals and didn't get adequate feedback to correct earlier in the process. I also realized that the impact of emotions were excluded from the productivity equation. So I created a new equation for productivity.

The funficient way aims to help you put the fun back into efficiency by focusing on the right thing at the right time.

Productivity explained.

Most productivity improvements in knowledge work environments focus on efficiency.

Many teams measure productivity by looking at the tangible things like effort, competence, and environmental factors. Typically, to increase the capacity they will add more people to the team. They might tend to hire senior team members in the hope to increase competence, and they tend to invest in expensive equipment and productivity tools to make the work environment more productive.

Other useful metrices include lead time (the time it takes to release a single feature), the deployment frequency (how often do you release new features to production), and the other DORA metrics.

Although all these measures are useful, they all assume that you are confident that you are indeed building the right thing. But are you really sure that what you are investing your effort and competence in is what the customers want and need?

Software is, however, different from manufacturing - where most of the original productivity measures originated from - in that we produce things that doesn't exist. That means you are never sure that what you are creating meets customer expectations until you can give it to the customer to validate.

What if we're measuring the wrong thing?

Having worked with many teams, I realized that what causes teams to be less productive is rarely the resources. Adding more, as you would in a typical manufacturing environment, introduces complexity that slows down rather than speed up production. Increasing releases also means little if it isn't what the customer asked for, or add complexity to the workflow.

What most teams lack that decrease their productivity is a lack of clarity on the goal and too long (or short) feedback loops to validate that they are on the right track.

Without being clear on what you are building, and having everyone in the team agree on what this thing is, greatly increases your wasted effort and thus your productivity.

An even more important aspect of productivity mostly overlooked as it is so hard for most, is the impact of motivation. It's not the tools or the skills that make a great team, but the strength of the relationships. A demotivated team will most likely not invest much energy in relationships, and without strong relationships, you are as weak as the weakest link in your team.

To create hyper productive teams, you need clarity of your goal, regular and meaningful feedback loops that result in action, and an emphasis on emotions and what motivates people within a team, summarized in the equation below:

Productivity² = goal × feedback × motivation²

To maximize your productivity, spend more time gaining clarity and alignment on your goal. Design feedback loops that is both scientific and meaningful to the customers. Measuring analytics, for example, is useful to test a hypothesis, but it is more useful to speak directly to a select few customers to uncover unmet needs.

If you don't already, invest time in discovering what motivates your team and what their strenghts are. There is little things more motivating for an individual than allowing them to use their strenghts. Also make space for emotions and embrace the ups and downs of the life journey and include a coaching program to invest in motivation.
