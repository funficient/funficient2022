I'm Kate!

After spending years what felt like running around in circles getting nowhere slowly, I embarked on a personal journey to discover what it takes for people and teams to be more productive, make better decisions, and ultimately have a more fulfilling life.



Where it all started

While working in different organizations, I noticed the same patterns that keep teams from being productive. People seemed busy, yet releases were slow and error-prone.

Customers were frustrated by the amount of expensive and slow rework needed to get their desired outcomes. Team members were demotivated having to build the ever changing requirements and never given the time to deal with technical debt. Business owners felt powerless and furstrated not able to deliver reliably. There was clearly no winner.

The outcome was a lot of very expensive and time consuming wasted effort. It dawned on me that the answer was simple. It's not so much about speed or efficiency, but about accuracy.

The funficient way was born to help you optimize your most precious resource. Time.

Productivity explained.

Most productivity improvements in knowledge work environments focus only on efficiency.

Many teams measure productivity by looking at tangible things like effort, competence, and environmental factors. Typically, to increase capacity they add more people. Or they hire senior team members in the hope to increase competence, and tend to invest in expensive equipment and productivity tools to make the work environment more productive.

Useful metrics include lead time (the time it takes to release a single feature), the deployment frequency (how often you release new features to production), and other DORA metrics.

And although these measures are useful, they all assume you are building the right thing. But are you confident that your effort is being investing in is what the users really want and need? What if you could reduce development time and cost simply by making sure your team is working on the most important thing?

Productivity is, after all, less about efficiency and more about accuracy. Efficiency only becomes useful when you produce the same artifact repeatedly. The key to productivity in software development is getting clear on the desired outcome.

What if we're measuring the wrong thing?

Resources are rarely the cause of productivity issues. Adding more, as in a typical manufacturing environment, introduces complexity that slows down rather than speed up production. Productivity decreases mainly when there is a lack of clarity on the goal and not adequate feedback loops to validate the concept or hypothesis early.

When the whole team isn't clear and agree on the single most important thing to build is next, you greatly increase your wasted effort. A clear, collaborative goal is the starting point for increased productivity.

The impact of emotions on productivity is also a vital, often overlooked, key to productivity. It's not the tools or the skills that make a great team, but the strenght of their relationships. Without emotions and human to human connection you are as weak as the weakest link in your team.

To create hyper productive teams, you first and foremost need clarity of the goal, regular and meaningful feedback loops, and responding to, rather than suppress emotions.

Productivity² = goal × alignment x feedback × motivation²

To maximize your productivity, spend more time gaining clarity and alignment on your goal. Design feedback loops that is both scientific and meaningful to the customers. Measuring analytics, for example, is useful to test a hypothesis, but it is more useful to speak directly to a select few customers to uncover unmet needs.

If you don't already, invest time in discovering what motivates your team and what their strenghts are. There is little things more motivating for an individual than allowing them to use their strenghts. And make space for emotions. Embrace the ups and downs of the life and work journey.
